## 1969-03-01 & 1969-03-02: Fillmore West, San Francisco

### The Bill
- **Grateful Dead** (headliner)
- **Pentangle** (British acoustic group: John Renbourn, Bert Jansch, Danny Thompson, Terry Cox, Jacqui McShee)
- **Sir Douglas Quintet** (Doug Sahm; may have been replaced by **Frumious Bandersnatch** on March 1 due to Sahm illness)
- **Shades of Joy** (Martin Fierro on sax/flute, Jackie King on guitar, Millie Foster vocals, Jymm Young keyboards, Edward Adams bass, Jose Rodriguez drums)
- **Jim Rinehart** (juggler)
- This was a 4-night run: Feb 27, 28, Mar 1, 2 — the source material for **Live/Dead**

### Band Lineup
Jerry Garcia (guitar), Phil Lesh (bass), Bob Weir (guitar), Bill Kreutzmann (drums), Mickey Hart (drums), Tom Constanten (piano/organ), Pigpen (vocals, congas, blues)

### KEY NEWSPAPER REVIEW — Ralph Gleason, SF Chronicle, March 3, 1969
**"On The Town" column** — Gleason attended Feb 27 (first set):
- Dead were **"absolutely wonderful"** with **"one of the most individual sounds of any band"**
- Despite **technical stage problems**, they were **"warm, mellow and moving"**
- Praised the instrumental passages between Garcia and Lesh
- Performed "That's It for the Other One" from *Aoxomoxoa*
- Tom Constanten described as "Mills College alumnus" on piano
- Pentangle "captivated the musicians present" — Dead members came out from backstage to listen
- Their acoustic sound produced **"a huge guitar sound"** with **"no distortion"**
- Sir Douglas performed "Mendocino" and "She's About a Mover," "really delightful," got everybody dancing
- Shades of Joy featured **"wild free form modern jazz saxophone playing by Martin Fierro, a roaring R&B rhythm section"**
- SOURCE: http://deadsources.blogspot.com/2013/06/february-27-1969-fillmore-west.html

### Recording Significance
- **16-track recording** — first live rock album recorded in 16-track
- 5 of 7 songs on *Live/Dead* came from these four nights: "Dark Star," "St. Stephen," "Death Don't Have No Mercy," "Feedback," "And We Bid You Goodnight"
- Complete run released as 10-CD box set *Fillmore West 1969: The Complete Recordings* (2005, limited to 10,000 copies)
- SOURCE: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fillmore_West_1969:_The_Complete_Recordings

### Pentangle's Influence on the Dead — STRONG FOR REVIEWS
- **Garcia explicitly stated a decade later** that hearing Pentangle made him consider "two amplified acoustic guitars over a rock rhythm section as a sonic possibility"
- Pentangle was **the catalyst** for the Dead's acoustic format that would break out almost a year later (leading to *Workingman's Dead* and *American Beauty*)
- The Fillmore format required headline acts to remain in-house during all performances, ensuring the Dead heard Pentangle perform multiple sets
- SOURCE: http://lostlivedead.blogspot.com/2010/03/fillmore-west-february-27-march-2-1969.html

### Robert Christgau — New York Times, July 27, 1969
- Broader article about the Dead's rise: **"The Grateful Dead Are Rising Again"**
- Garcia quote: **"We get hard fans. Once we play the same place three or four times we know we have a core of fans that'll stay with us no matter what we do."**
- Christgau described a Flushing Meadow show: nearly 5,000 fans, **"one of those titanic sets we'd always dreamed about"**
- SOURCE: https://www.robertchristgau.com/xg/news/grateful-69.php

### Christgau Album Reviews
- *Live/Dead* (1969): **Grade A+** — **"Side two of this four-sided set contains the finest rock improvisation ever recorded, and the rest is gently transcendent as usual. Beautifully recorded, too."**
- *Aoxomoxoa* (1969): Grade A — "One experimental cut which hasn't made it for me yet, otherwise fantastic"
- SOURCE: https://www.robertchristgau.com/get_artist.php?name=Grateful+Dead

### Venue Details — Fillmore West
- Bill Graham's premier West Coast venue
- Multi-band format: three acts each performing twice during the evening
- On Fridays and Saturdays, a fourth opening act played a single set to extend the show
- SOURCE: http://lostlivedead.blogspot.com/2011/03/february-28-1969-fillmore-west-grateful.html

## 1971-02-18 through 1971-02-24: Capitol Theater, Port Chester, NY (6 nights)

### Venue Architecture & History — STRONG FOR REVIEWS
- Designed by noted theater architect **Thomas W. Lamb**, built in **1926**
- **65-foot domed ceiling**, hand-carved moldings with **271 squirrels**, elegant balconies, chandeliers
- Opening night in 1926 sold out all **2,000 seats** and turned hundreds away
- Featured **the only theater refrigerating system in Westchester County**
- Auditorium similar to Lamb's Fox Savoy Theatre in Brooklyn: domed ceiling, side-wall boxes, draped archways
- Added to **National Register of Historic Places** in 1984
- **Capacity: approximately 1,850-2,000**
- SOURCE: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capitol_Theatre_(Port_Chester,_New_York)
- SOURCE: https://www.thecapitoltheatre.com/about/history

### Promoter: Howard Stein
- Took over operation approximately one year before (early 1970)
- **Port Chester city council had been trying to close the theater** through restraining orders and ordinances, including a rule prohibiting live entertainment after 1 AM
- Stein had "booked some of the country's better bands, built up a steady regular audience, and spent about half his time fighting city hall"
- Planned summer venue at Flushing Meadow Park in Queens for "mini-festivals"
- SOURCE: http://deadsources.blogspot.com/2012/07/1971-capitol-theater-port-chester.html (Rolling Stone, April 1, 1971)

### Band State
- **First shows without Mickey Hart** — Hart was absent ("under the weather" per Weir, had quit)
- Bill Kreutzmann beginning **four-year tenure as sole drummer**
- Band was **"on the verge of cleaning up our debts"** (Weir)
- Minimal rehearsal: **"we just got a new rehearsal studio, but up to now we haven't rehearsed a whole hell of a lot, in fact, very little"** (Weir)
- SOURCE: http://deadsources.blogspot.com/2012/07/february-1971-capitol-theater-bob-weir.html (Harvard Independent, March 4 & 11, 1971)

### What Was on the Bill
- **New Riders of the Purple Sage** opened every night
- NRPS lineup: Spencer Dryden (ex-Jefferson Airplane) on drums, Dave Torbert bass, Dave Nelson guitar, John Dawson guitar, **Jerry Garcia on pedal steel guitar**
- A **Hell's Angel named Mario played tambourine** throughout both nights (at least Feb 19-20)
- SOURCE: Harvard Independent, March 4 & 11, 1971

### Alembic Recording
- **Alembic Sound recorded the entire week** for what became a live Dead album
- Expected to be a 2-record set released "probably in a couple of months" (actually took until September — became *Skull & Roses*)
- SOURCE: Harvard Independent interview

### KEY DETAILS BY NIGHT:

#### February 18, 1971 — MASSIVE NIGHT
- **Five original songs debuted**: "Bertha," "Greatest Story Ever Told," "Loser," "Playing in the Band," and "Wharf Rat"
- **"Beautiful Jam"** during Dark Star > Wharf Rat > Dark Star sequence — historically significant
- Radio personality David Gans later played this jam for Phil Lesh in 1997, **moving him to tears**
- Pigpen's blues influence central to show's emotional impact
- **BOMB THREAT**: Anonymous caller reported bomb; New Riders were performing when firefighters and Dead manager **Sam Cutler** cleared the hall. Audience resisted evacuation. After brief search, declared hoax. **Crowd of several hundred "grinning crashers" streamed back in without tickets.**
- Stein suspected the threat was planted to document fire code violations
- SOURCE: http://deadsources.blogspot.com/2012/07/1971-capitol-theater-port-chester.html
- SOURCE: https://www.thecapitoltheatre.com/blog/detail/6-of-the-most-legendary-grateful-dead-shows-that-happened-at-the-cap-47-years-ago

#### February 19, 1971
- First performances of **"Bird Song"** and **"Deal"** — two songs the band would play for the rest of their career
- Bill Kreutzmann's first night as sole drummer
- SOURCE: Capitol Theatre blog

#### February 20, 1971 — ESP EXPERIMENT
- **Dream telepathy experiment** led by **Dr. Stanley Krippner** (Maimonides Medical Center, Brooklyn)
- English psychic **Malcolm Bessent** attempted to receive "mind to mind transmissions" from 2,000-person audience while in a sleep lab 45 miles away
- **14 art slides** pre-selected; 2 chosen randomly per night using dice
- Target image this night: **"Philosophy of the Boudoir" by Magritte** (headless woman in transparent gown)
- Bessent slept with electrodes monitoring REM sleep, awakened 10 minutes after REM detection for dream reports
- **Results: judges correctly matched dreams to art prints on 4 of 6 nights** — only 1% chance of occurring by coincidence
- Experiment compromised by audience members already being in altered states
- SOURCE: https://stanleykrippner.weebly.com/a-pilot-study-in-dream-telepathy-with-the-grateful-dead.html
- SOURCE: https://www.thecapitoltheatre.com/blog/detail/6-of-the-most-legendary-grateful-dead-shows-that-happened-at-the-cap-47-years-ago

#### February 21, 1971
- Powerful rendition of "Easy Wind" showcasing Pigpen's signature grit
- China Cat Sunflower > I Know You Rider
- Extended 17-minute "Good Lovin'" with Drums section, flowing into "Uncle John's Band"
- SOURCE: Capitol Theatre blog

#### February 23, 1971
- High-energy performance following night off
- "The Other One" provided exploratory improvisation
- "Bird Song" served as tribute to Janis Joplin (connections to Pigpen and the band)
- SOURCE: Capitol Theatre blog

#### February 24, 1971 — FINAL NIGHT
- "Casey Jones" and "Hard to Handle"
- Nearly 20-minute "Good Lovin' > Drums" segment
- **"Turn on Your Lovelight"** — potentially one of the finest versions ever performed
- Bob Weir addressed bomb threat aftermath: thanked whoever called it in, noting they **"got in free"**
- SOURCE: Capitol Theatre blog
- SOURCE: http://deadsources.blogspot.com/2012/07/1971-capitol-theater-port-chester.html

### KEY NEWSPAPER REVIEW — Georgetown Hoya, March 4, 1971 (Ray Trifari)
- **"Once again the Dead asserted that they are probably the best American band"**
- **"the music created blends and mixes so perfectly that it sounds like a studio recording"**
- Show "left the stage with the crowd at its feet for 20 minutes"
- Advertised as "a showcase for new material"
- Songs mentioned: "Casey Jones," "Truckin'," "Sugar Magnolia," "Johnny B. Goode," "Dark Star" (30-minute version), "Uncle John's Band"
- Unusual: reviewer from Georgetown (DC) who traveled 250+ miles, then **taped the show and broadcast it on Georgetown's WGTB-FM**
- SOURCE: http://deadsources.blogspot.com/2019/08/february-18-1971-capitol-theater-port.html

### Bob Weir Interview — Harvard Independent, March 4 & 11, 1971
- On *Aoxomoxoa*: **"That whole album was a waste in many respects and an invaluable experience in other respects"**
- On Tom Constanten: **"he has absolutely no background [in rock and roll]...it occurred to us...that he wasn't really a rock and roll musician"**
- On Vintage Dead bootleg: **"at first we asked each other 'who the fuck is that'"** — initially thought it was studio musicians
- Warner Bros. edited "Uncle John's Band" for radio, removing a verse: **"they hacked it to ribbons"**
- On gate crashers: **"once you start piling people on top of one another it becomes a bummer"**
- On Dead's atonal work: **"We go through several kinds of scales, into an atonal mode and out, and in and out of a whole bunch of different scales"**
- SOURCE: http://deadsources.blogspot.com/2012/07/february-1971-capitol-theater-bob-weir.html

### Garcia Interview — Jazz & Pop, February 1971 (conducted Oct/Nov 1970 at Holiday Inn, NJ)
- On Woodstock: **"We played such a bad set...we were all pretty smashed...couldn't see one of them [audience members]...blue sparks were flying"**
- On Altamont: **"Altamont was such a bummer, man. You could just feel the tension in the air"**
- On the Trans-Canadian train trip: **"the best time I've had in rock and roll...jam sessions all the way across Canada"**
- On Janis Joplin's death: **"It was just a little too much...just an accident, a dumb fucking accident...she went out when she was happy"**
- On growing popularity: **"It's a mystery to me...we haven't really changed our scene materially"**
- SOURCE: http://deadsources.blogspot.com/2013/11/february-1971-jerry-garcia-interview.html

### Ken & Judy Lee — Capitol Theatre Archivists
- Worked at the Cap during 1970s heyday
- Equipment: **Sony TC 124 cassette recorder, two Sony F 95 microphones** with 10-meter extension cables
- Microphones placed **"like a pair of ears, a 45-degree angle towards the stage"**
- Acoustic foam cocoons around microphones to filter noise
- Each tape side allowed only 45 minutes — Judy managed critical tape flips
- Their freely shared recordings shaped music education and professional careers
- On the Cap's magic: **"the sense of family and being in our second home"**
- **Harry Howard Hook & Ladder Co. #1** (local fire department) became notable Deadheads, inducted Phil Lesh as honorary member
- SOURCE: https://www.thecapitoltheatre.com/blog/detail/port-chester-legends-recorded-every-grateful-dead-show-at-the-cap-1

## 1971-04-04 & 1971-04-06: Manhattan Center, NYC

### Venue Architecture & History — STRONG FOR REVIEWS
- **311 West 34th Street**, originally the **Manhattan Opera House**, built by **Oscar Hammerstein I** in 1906
- In 1922, purchased by **Ancient Accepted Scottish Rite of Free Masonry**, who built new facade and **Grand Ballroom on the seventh floor**
- Grand Ballroom: **10,000 square feet, 40-foot high vaulted ceilings**
- In 1926, Warner Bros. rented the ballroom to set up the **Vitaphone sound-on-disc system** — used to record the New York Philharmonic for *Don Juan*, the first commercial film with recorded musical soundtrack
- Aquamarine walls with **social-themed murals**, two enormous balconies
- **Capacity: approximately 2,000-2,500**
- Later purchased by Unification Church in 1976 for $3 million
- SOURCE: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manhattan_Center

### The "Dance Marathon" Concept
- Advertised as the **"first annual" Grateful Dead Dance Marathon**
- Three nights: April 4, 5, 6, 1971
- **Ticket price: $5**
- Promoted as "the Dead would play as long as the audience would continue to dance"
- In reality, functioned as standard concert — after six hours came "Uncle John's Band" and the end

### KEY SOURCE — The New Yorker, April 17, 1971
- New Yorker **sent a correspondent to the final night**
- **"The promoters had oversold the hall, so that while there was room in some parts of the room for rhythmic breathing, dancing was rarely a possibility"**
- Described the dance-marathon hype as "a little bit embarrassing"
- Called it **"an unnecessary hype from a band with the kind of New York following that the Dead have"**
- SOURCE: http://deadsources.blogspot.com/2012/07/april-6-1971-manhattan-center-new-york.html

### Catastrophic Overcrowding
- **Capacity 2,000-2,500; actual attendance approximately 5,000-10,000**
- Promoter **Howard Stein** oversold tickets
- Security personnel **illegally selling tickets through side entrances**
- Counterfeit tickets in circulation
- Unauthorized break-ins including **attempts through the roof**
- People described needing to **"move away towards the back just so they could breathe"**
- Standing room only — no seats available, no room to dance despite "dance marathon" billing
- SOURCE: http://deadsources.blogspot.com/2019/08/february-4-1971-manhattan-center-new.html

### Venue Description (New York Post / Alfred Aronowitz, April 19, 1971)
- Stage bathed in **purple and multicolored lights**
- Audience dressed in **"new mufti" — layered T-shirts, sweatshirts, flannel, Army jackets, overalls**
- **"Low-key trust" atmosphere** — people lay on floors without being trampled
- No anarchic celebration or "Oh, wow" exclamations — a more subdued, knowing crowd
- Coat checkroom operated on **"sound anarchistic principles"** without a checker
- SOURCE: http://deadsources.blogspot.com/2012/07/april-6-1971-manhattan-center-new-york.html

### Performance Details
- **Opening act**: New Riders of the Purple Sage (Garcia on pedal steel)
- **Duration**: approximately six hours of music total each night
- **Band**: Grateful Dead without Mickey Hart; Bill Kreutzmann sole drummer
- Songs: "Morning Dew," "Easy Wind," "Good Lovin'," "Me and Bobby McGee," "Johnny B. Goode," "St. Stephen," "Not Fade Away," "Truckin'," "Sugar Magnolia," "Casey Jones," "Uncle John's Band" (closer)
- Bob Weir received particular praise for vocal performance and stage presence
- Dead's recorded material "sound[ed] very weak" compared to live performance
- SOURCE: Observer (Case Western Reserve University), April 9, 1971

### Howard Stein's Apology (April 15, 1971)
- **Formally apologized** for overselling the ballroom
- Blamed security personnel for illegally selling tickets at side entrances
- Acknowledged counterfeit tickets and unauthorized break-ins
- SOURCE: http://deadsources.blogspot.com/2012/07/april-6-1971-manhattan-center-new-york.html

### Skull & Roses Recording
- Material from these shows made it onto the album:
  - **April 5**: "Big Railroad Blues," "Not Fade Away / Goin' Down the Road Feeling Bad"
  - **April 6**: "Playing in the Band," "Oh, Boy!," "I'm a Hog for You"
- Sixteen-track recording system; control booth "little larger than a closet"
- **"The 'best' of some sixty hours of tape"** were selected for the album
- Poorly ventilated venue, 95-degree heat, four hours non-stop each night
- Garcia "charging at neck-breaking speed thru endless variations" on unplugged guitar before show
- Phil Lesh attempted explaining sixteen-track equipment to a reporter
- Crew: Ramrod, Jackson, and Sonny Heard managed stage logistics
- SOURCE: http://deadsources.blogspot.com/2012/07/april-1971-recording-live-album.html

## 1971-04-07 & 1971-04-08: Music Hall, Boston

### Venue Architecture & History — STRONG FOR REVIEWS
- Built in **1852** with a $100,000 donation from the **Harvard Musical Association**
- Architect: **George Snell**, assisted by Alpheus C. Morse
- Dimensions: **130 feet long x 78 feet wide x 65 feet high**
- Acoustics engineered under guidance from **John Scott Russell**
- Gleason's Pictorial declared it **"an ornament to our city, and is probably unsurpassed for architectural excellence and beauty in this country"**
- Home to the **first concert pipe organ in the United States** (1862): built by E.F. Walcker of Ludwigsburg, Germany — 5,474 pipes, 84 registers
- On **December 31, 1862**, Northern abolitionists gathered here on the eve of the Emancipation Proclamation — **Frederick Douglass, Harriet Beecher Stowe, William Lloyd Garrison, and Harriet Tubman** attended
- Boston Symphony performed here until moving to Symphony Hall in 1900
- Renamed **Orpheum Theatre** in 1906; closed as movie theatre January 31, 1971
- Reopened as **the Aquarius** (live concert hall) on May 27, 1971 with James Brown
- NOTE: The Dead played here April 7-8, 1971, when it was still called "Music Hall" — between its life as the Orpheum movie theater and before the Aquarius reopening
- SOURCE: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boston_Music_Hall
- SOURCE: https://www.nps.gov/places/boston-music-hall.htm

### KEY NEWSPAPER REVIEW — Jon Landau, Boston Phoenix, April 1971 — DEVASTATING
This is one of the most scathing contemporary reviews of the Grateful Dead ever published.

- Called the performance **"a disaster"**
- Sound problems: **"the sound was unmixed — you couldn't hear the bass until halfway through"** when someone finally turned it up
- Vocals: **"the vocalists butchered everything in sight"** with at least one member singing **"consistently off key"**
- Overall: **"Their instrumental work was uninspired, their performing attitude sterile"**
- BUT: Garcia was **"an unquestionably fine guitarist"** yet **"his singing was horrid"**
- Audience demographics: **"Where Three Dog Night gets high school students, the Dead gets the dropouts"**
- Crowd behavior included **"enough manic dizziness, enough mindless reacting, and enough dope to last this city for the next six months"**
- Contrast: **Other reviews of these same shows called them "incredible" with "packed and ecstatic" audiences**
- Landau attended the Fillmore East shows later and doubled down: **"they were every bit as bad at the Fillmore as they were at the Music Hall"**
- Criticized the band's **"marathon consciousness"** and inability to **"edit themselves"**
- Called Bill Kreutzmann insufficient following Mickey Hart's departure
- Later reprinted in Landau's 1972 book *It's Too Late to Stop Now* in the chapter **"White Rock: Three Dog Night and the Grateful Dead"**
- SOURCE: http://deadsources.blogspot.com/2021/07/aprilmay-1971-boston-music-hall.html

### Show Details
- April 7 was short due to **curfew restrictions**
- Songs mentioned: Me and My Uncle, Casey Jones, Sugar Magnolia, China Cat Sunflower
- New Riders of the Purple Sage opened
- SOURCE: https://jerrygarcia.com/show/1971-04-07-boston-music-hall-boston-ma-usa/

## 1971-04-26: Fillmore East, NYC (Duane Allman sits in)

### Venue Architecture & History — STRONG FOR REVIEWS
- **105 Second Avenue**, near East 6th Street, Lower East Side, Manhattan
- Originally built as a **Yiddish theater in 1925-1926** (the Commodore), designed by **Harrison Wiseman** in Medieval Revival style
- **Narrow brick facade**, accented by a **domed roof** contributing to distinctive skyline presence
- Bill Graham's rock venue, open **March 8, 1968 to June 27, 1971**
- Seated **2,654 people**
- **Joshua Light Show** (headed by Joshua White) was integral to performances — psychedelic art lighting on backdrop behind bands
- Building **demolished in 1997**; loft building with Second Avenue lobby still exists (now a bank)
- SOURCE: https://www.fillmoreeastrecon.com/
- SOURCE: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fillmore_East

### Why the Fillmore Closed — Bill Graham's Open Letter
- Published in **Village Voice, April 29, 1971** (same week as the Dead's run!)
- Cited **decline in quality of acts** and **musical sophistication of audience**
- Tired of **"the abuse of many members of the public and press"**
- Chief complaint: **"the unreasonable and totally destructive inflation of the live concert scene"**
- Acts graduating to arenas/stadiums holding 10,000+ — couldn't compete on pay
- Final concert: June 27, 1971 — Allman Brothers Band headlined (with Albert King, who'd played opening night)
- SOURCE: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fillmore_East

### The Five-Night Run: April 25-29, 1971
- Material recorded for **Ladies and Gentlemen... the Grateful Dead** (released 2000, 4-CD set)
- Run grossed **$63,500** with capacity attendance (per Variety, May 5, 1971)
- New Riders of the Purple Sage opened each night

### April 26 — Duane Allman Sits In
- **Duane Allman joined for three songs** to start the second set:
  1. **"Sugar Magnolia"** — Garcia and Allman "take flight together...playing with graceful ferocity"
  2. **"It Hurts Me Too"** — Allman's slide guitar dominated this Elmore James cover; "classic performance, Duane is in his element, wailing the blues"
  3. **"Beat It On Down The Line"**
- **This was the last time Duane Allman performed with the Grateful Dead** — he died in a motorcycle accident October 29, 1971
- Phil Lesh quote: **"Jerry had an immense amount of respect for Duane"** — watching Duane play was **"like watching a flower grow"**
- Duane Allman: **"I love the Dead!"** — described Garcia as someone who **"could walk on water"**
- Dickey Betts on shared philosophy: **"The Dead's philosophy was always very similar to ours...keeping music honest and fun and trying to make it a transcendental experience for the audience"**
- SOURCE: https://alanpaul.substack.com/p/brothers-of-the-road-more-on-the

### Skull & Roses Tracks from April 26
- **"Mama Tried," "Big Boss Man," "Wharf Rat"** were recorded this night
- SOURCE: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grateful_Dead_(album)

## 1971-12-14 & 1971-12-15: Hill Auditorium, Ann Arbor

### Venue Architecture & History — STRONG FOR REVIEWS
- University of Michigan campus, Ann Arbor
- Designed by **Albert Kahn** (with Ernest Wilby), completed **1913**
- Named after **Arthur Hill**, UM regent whose **$200,000 bequest** made it possible
- When opened, hailed as **"a monument to perfect acoustics"**
- Acoustics by collaboration with **Hugh Tallant** (NY architect/acoustical engineer)
- **Unique parabolic ceiling** — auditorium shaped like a megaphone
- Exterior: **Chicago School of Architecture**; interior: **Arts and Crafts style** in warm hues
- Influenced by **Louis Sullivan's Auditorium Building** in Chicago; **Prairie Style** with diaper brick designs and red tile roof
- **Capacity: approximately 3,500-4,100**
- $38.6 million renovation completed 2004
- SOURCE: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hill_Auditorium
- SOURCE: https://smtd.umich.edu/facilities/hill-auditorium/

### Band State — Critical Transition
- **Keith Godchaux** joined in October 1971 — two months before these shows
- **Pigpen returned** after serious health problems had sidelined him since August
- First time the band had both a keyboardist and Pigpen together in the new configuration
- Godchaux "fully settled into his role" by Ann Arbor — "reshaping the band's rhythm section and inspiring play at all levels"
- This was **"the distinct sound of metamorphosis"** — the beast of 1972 was already taking form
- SOURCE: https://zencowpoke.substack.com/p/grateful-deadhill-auditorium-ann-5ad
- SOURCE: https://www.deadlistening.com/2010/04/1971-december-15-ann-arbor.html

### The FM Broadcast Tour Context
- Warner Bros. allocated approximately **$100,000** to fund FM broadcasts across 14 cities during fall 1971 tour
- Averaging **$7,000 per broadcast**, from band's future royalties
- **Ann Arbor notably had NO FM broadcast** despite hosting Dead shows
- Nearby broadcasts: Detroit (WABX-fm, Oct 23 at Easttown Theater), Chicago (WGLD-fm, Oct 21-22)
- Broadcast strategy: Following violence at Boston's Music Hall, Dead arranged radio broadcasts to **allow fans to hear concerts without venue overcrowding and associated security problems**
- SOURCE: http://lostlivedead.blogspot.com/2015/03/grateful-dead-live-fm-broadcasts-1971.html

### December 1971 Tour Context — Cash Box Review (December 25, 1971)
- Reviewer observed a troubling shift: **"communication lines between performer and listener are down"** despite high energy

- Repeated requests for familiar songs **"heard at least ninety times previously"**

### Felt Forum Context (December 4-7, nearby shows)
- Four sold-out nights, **$5.50 top ticket price**, grossed approximately $80,000
- WNEW-FM broadcast December 5: **8 PM to 2:30 AM**, uninterrupted — audience estimated at **3-5 million listeners**
- Keith Godchaux's piano **"not heard often because of weak miking"**
- Pigpen **"seemed in much better form"** on December 7 — "tough and gritty"
- Dead performed approximately **4 hours 15 minutes** with one 15-minute break
- Warner Brothers and Columbia Records shared approximately $8,000 in broadcasting fees
- SOURCE: http://deadsources.blogspot.com/2020/12/december-5-1971-felt-forum-nyc.html
